# Policy Q&A Guardrails

## Goal
Define when an HR policy workflow can answer a question directly and when it should escalate.

## Safe answer conditions
A policy workflow can usually answer when:
- the answer is clearly stated in the handbook or policy text
- the question is informational, not case-specific
- the workflow can quote the relevant source section
- there is low risk if the answer is incomplete

## Escalate conditions
The workflow should escalate when:
- policy language is ambiguous or conflicting
- the question involves a real employee situation with possible risk
- the issue touches leave, accommodations, discipline, investigations, pay, or termination
- the user is asking for interpretation beyond the written policy
- the workflow cannot identify a reliable source section

## Good answer format
1. Plain-English summary  
2. Exact policy section referenced  
3. Any uncertainty or limitation  
4. Whether human follow-up is recommended

## Bad answer behaviors
- pretending certainty where the policy is vague
- fabricating policy language
- giving legal advice
- skipping escalation on sensitive issues
- answering from general HR knowledge instead of the actual policy source

## Example
Question: "Can a manager deny intermittent leave if the team is short staffed?"

Bad workflow behavior:
- gives a confident yes/no from generic knowledge

Better workflow behavior:
- identifies whether the handbook even covers this
- quotes the relevant leave language if present
- flags that a case-specific leave decision requires human review

## Why this matters
Policy Q&A feels simple until the question becomes real. Guardrails are what keep a helpful workflow from becoming a risky one.
