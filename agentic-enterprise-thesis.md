# Agentic Enterprise Thesis

## Core idea
I think the long-term opportunity is bigger than adding AI features to existing SaaS products.

My bet is that the enterprise gradually shifts from buying more disconnected software toward designing company-specific agentic systems that help run the business.

In that world, traditional SaaS becomes thinner. It starts to look more like an interface layer, a data layer, or a utility layer rather than the full operating model.

## What I think happens next
Over the next few years, more companies will start building their own agentic workflow systems on top of their internal processes, policies, data, and approvals.

That means the durable advantage shifts toward:
- workflow design
- orchestration
- context management
- approval systems
- auditability
- privacy and access control
- company-specific operating logic

The important question becomes less "which SaaS tool do we buy?" and more "how do we design the system that helps run this company?"

## SaaS as the thinner layer
I do not think SaaS disappears.

I think it gets compressed.

A lot of software will still exist, but more of it will become:
- a system of record
- a user interface
- a data service
- a specialized capability provider
- a component inside a larger agentic operating layer

That is different from the current model where the SaaS application is often the primary system and the workflow has to bend around it.

## Why I think this happens
Because most enterprise work is not just data entry or reporting. It is coordination.

It involves:
- intake
- handoffs
- policy checks
- routing
- approvals
- exceptions
- judgment
- follow-up
- documentation

Those are workflow problems before they are software-category problems.

Agentic systems are much better aligned to workflow logic than traditional static SaaS screens are.

## On-prem and private deployment
I also think there will be a meaningful shift back toward private and on-prem deployment for a lot of enterprise AI work.

Not because cloud disappears, but because privacy, control, latency, reliability, and cost will matter more as companies move from experimentation into real operations.

The more sensitive the function, the stronger that pressure becomes.

For many enterprises, the desirable future state will be:
- private context
- controlled models
- internal workflow logic
- explicit approvals
- clear audit trails
- selective use of frontier cloud models when they are truly needed

## Local model view
My current bet is that within the next three years, local models become powerful enough to handle the majority of enterprise work.

Not necessarily the hardest edge cases.
Not necessarily the final decision in every high-stakes situation.
But the majority of day-to-day workflow work:
- summarization
- extraction
- classification
- drafting
- policy retrieval
- triage
- routing
- structured follow-up
- repetitive analysis

That changes the economics and architecture of the whole stack.

## What stays in the cloud
I do not think cloud models go away.

I think they remain important for:
- frontier reasoning
- hard edge cases
- overflow capacity
- specialized multimodal work
- situations where the best available model quality is worth the tradeoff

But I think the center of gravity moves toward hybrid systems, with more work happening locally or in controlled environments than many people currently expect.

## Why HR matters in this shift
HR is one of the clearest early domains for this broader transition.

Why?
Because HR work is full of:
- recurring workflows
- approvals
- sensitive context
- privacy boundaries
- legal and policy constraints
- documentation needs
- audit requirements

That makes HR a strong fit for company-specific agentic systems and a weak fit for careless black-box autonomy.

## What this means for OpenClaw in HR
For me, OpenClaw is interesting because it points toward workflow systems, not just chatbot experiences.

The real opportunity is not autonomous HR decision-making.
It is building systems that can:
- gather context
- surface policy
- structure intake
- route approvals
- preserve audit trails
- keep humans in the loop
- operate inside real privacy and control boundaries

## My bet
My bet is that the next important enterprise software shift is not just "AI inside SaaS."

It is the emergence of company-specific agentic operating systems, with SaaS becoming thinner, local/private models becoming more capable, and workflow design becoming the real strategic layer.

And I think HR will be one of the most important proving grounds for that shift.
