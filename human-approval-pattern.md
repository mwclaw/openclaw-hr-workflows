# Human Approval Pattern

## Goal
Define where an HR workflow must pause for human review before action is taken.

## Why this matters
HR workflows often touch pay, leave, discipline, accommodations, performance, investigations, and employee relations. In those areas, automation without review creates unnecessary risk.

## Principle
An HR agent can prepare, summarize, route, flag, or draft.  
A human should approve any action that could materially affect a person, their employment status, or a sensitive workplace outcome.

## Good uses before approval
- summarize policy language
- collect relevant context
- draft a response for review
- identify missing information
- recommend escalation
- prepare next-step options

## Must-pause approval points
A human should review before:
- sending sensitive employee-facing communication
- recommending discipline or termination-related action
- interpreting leave or accommodation situations beyond clear policy text
- making pay, promotion, or leveling recommendations
- answering questions involving protected class, harassment, retaliation, or legal exposure
- finalizing any high-stakes compliance decision

## Approval log should capture
- what the workflow wanted to do
- why it paused
- who approved or rejected it
- timestamp of approval
- any edits made by the human
- final action taken

## Practical design rule
The workflow should make the human checkpoint obvious, not hidden. Approval should be a real state in the workflow, not a vague assumption.

## Why this is useful in HR
This pattern preserves speed without pretending that sensitive HR judgment can be safely automated end to end.
